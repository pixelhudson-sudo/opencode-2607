# Communication Strategy: Non-Renewal Notice to Terron Monroe
**Property:** 127 County Route 49, Slate Hill, NY 10973
**Date prepared:** April 2, 2026
**Situation:** Lease expired March 31, 2026. Tenant is holdover month-to-month. You intend to not renew and will substantially renovate. 90-day notice required.

---

## Before You Start

**Mindset:** This is not a negotiation. You are a property owner exercising a legal right. You are not ending a relationship — you are closing a tenancy. The renovation is real, the decision is final, and your job is to communicate it clearly without creating legal liability in the process.

**The #1 rule in everything you send:** Less is more. Every extra word is a potential exhibit in Housing Court. Say what is legally required, nothing more.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not mention the septic, the plumbing, or any repair issues in your notice or follow-up texts — this hands her the habitability argument on a silver platter
- Do not apologize — it implies wrongdoing
- Do not explain your renovation plans in detail — specifics can be challenged as pretextual
- Do not respond to her rent reduction request in any way
- Do not respond to her inspector threat in any way — treat it as if it was never said
- Do not negotiate in writing or verbally — any concession creates an implied agreement

**Timing:** Send the formal written notice first (certified mail). After it's in the mail, send the text. Never lead with text — the formal notice must be the official record.

**Why this wording protects you:**
- No reason given — none required in Wawayanda (Good Cause not applicable)
- Explicitly states acceptance of rent is NOT a renewal — prevents her arguing a new tenancy was created
- Cites the correct statute — shows procedural competence, harder to challenge
- Clean, neutral language — nothing that can be used as evidence of bad faith or retaliation

## Part 2 — The Follow-Up Text (Same Day, After Mail Is Sent)

Send this via iMessage after you have proof the certified mail was dropped off. Keep it short. This creates a timestamped digital record that she received notice.

**Text to send:**

> "Hi Terron. I'm sending you a formal notice today by certified mail regarding the tenancy at 127 County Route 49. Please watch for it. Let me know if you have any questions."

**That's it. Nothing else.**

Do not text the content of the notice. Do not explain the renovation. Do not respond to anything she says after this except to confirm you received her rent payment.

## Part 3 — If She Responds (Likely Scenarios & Exact Replies)

### If she says: "Why are you doing this? I've been a good tenant."
**Reply:**
> "I appreciate you as a tenant. This is a business decision related to plans I have for the property. The notice speaks for itself."

**Do not elaborate. Do not discuss the renovation details.**

---

### If she says: "I'm not leaving. I have rights."
**Reply:**
> "I understand. The notice is legally served per New York law. If you have questions about your rights, I'd encourage you to speak with an attorney."

**Do not argue. Do not threaten. Do not mention Housing Court yet.**

---

### If she says: "What about all the repairs that haven't been done? The bathroom, the mold, the pipes?"
**Reply:**
> "I'm aware of the ongoing repair work and have been actively addressing it throughout the tenancy. That is separate from this notice."

**Stop there. Do not list what you've done, do not promise future repairs, do not engage further on this topic.**

---

### If she says: "I'm going to call the health inspector / housing department."
**Reply:**
> "You're welcome to contact any agency you feel is appropriate."

**Dead stop. No more words. Do not get defensive. Do not explain anything.**

This response is intentionally neutral. Any defensive or explanatory response signals weakness and gives her information to use against you.

---

### If she says: "I'll pay you more rent / I'll fix the issues myself."
**Reply:**
> "Thank you, but my plans for the property don't allow for a lease renewal at this time."

**Do not counter-offer. Do not discuss rent amounts.**

---

### If she says: "Where am I supposed to go? I have a daughter living here."
**Reply:**
> "I understand this is difficult and I'm sorry for any hardship. I'm giving you 90 days to make arrangements, which is the full notice required by law."

This is the one moment where a brief acknowledgment of difficulty is appropriate — but only this much, no more. Acknowledge once, then hold the line.

---

### If she says: "I'm going to sue you."
**Reply:**
> "I've followed New York law in serving this notice. If you'd like to consult an attorney, that's your right."

**Do not say anything about lawsuits, settlements, or compensation.**

---

### If she goes silent and stops responding:
Do nothing. No follow-up texts. No calls. Let the certified mail do the work. Your legal obligation is served. The clock is running.

## Part 4 — During the 90-Day Period (April 3 – July 3, 2026)

**Rent payments:** Accept them. Reply only with:
> "Received. Thank you."

Nothing about month-to-month, holdover, or rent receipts via text. Keep your written receipt language for your own records.

**If she requests repairs during this period:**
Respond within 24 hours in writing. Keep it short:
> "Thank you for letting me know. I will arrange for [contractor/inspection] as soon as possible."

Then actually schedule it. Do not ignore repair requests during the notice period — this is the #1 thing that turns a clean non-renewal into a Housing Court nightmare. Ignoring repairs during active tenancy = willful neglect = her best argument.

**If she files a complaint with a government agency:**
Do not contact her about the complaint. Respond directly to the agency in writing, professionally, with your repair history documentation attached.

**Do not:**
- Visit the property unannounced — always give 24-hour written notice before entry (NY RPL §226-a)
- Disconnect any utilities or services
- Remove any appliances or fixtures
- Change locks
- Harass, intimidate, or pressure her to leave early
Any of the above = illegal lockout / constructive eviction = she wins in court and you pay her attorney's fees.

## Part 5 — If She Doesn't Leave by July 3

File a Holdover Petition in Orange County Court immediately — do not wait, do not negotiate, do not extend informally.

**Orange County Court (Landlord-Tenant):**
285 Main Street, Goshen, NY 10924
(845) 291-3111

Filing a petition does NOT mean immediate eviction. It starts the legal process. From filing to physical eviction is typically 3–6 months in Orange County if she contests. Budget for this.

Once you file, **all communication with her must go through the court process.** Stop texting her about the tenancy.

## Your Non-Negotiables

**Must have:** Her out of the property so you can begin renovation.

**Ideal outcome:** She leaves voluntarily by July 3, 2026 without any court involvement.

**Walk-away point:** If she files an HP Action or counterclaim, hand everything to a local landlord-tenant attorney immediately. Do not represent yourself in Housing Court.

**Red lines — never cross these regardless of pressure:**
- Never put any reason for non-renewal in writing other than "plans for the property"
- Never promise to fix specific items by specific dates in writing during the notice period
- Never offer a new lease or lease extension in writing or verbally
- Never accept rent past the termination date without a court's instruction
