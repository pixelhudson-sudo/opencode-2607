## The Science: How a Dog Dewormer Destroys Malignant Cells

Fenbendazole is a benzimidazole carbamate, same class as mebendazole (Albenza), which is FDA-approved for human use at $1,200–$4,500 per course. The molecular difference is negligible. The price difference is 100x.

Fenbendazole operates through four distinct anticancer mechanisms:

**Microtubule disruption.** Fenbendazole binds to β-tubulin at the colchicine binding site, inhibiting microtubule polymerization. Cancer cells are 10-100x more sensitive to tubulin disruption than healthy cells because of their rapid division rate. This is the same mechanism as vinca alkaloids like vinblastine — except those cost $500–$2,000 per dose and require IV administration in a hospital.

**Glucose uptake inhibition.** Fenbendazole blocks GLUT transporters, starving cancer cells of their primary fuel. Cancer cells consume 10-20x more glucose than normal cells (the Warburg effect). By cutting their fuel supply at the source, fenbendazole creates a metabolic bottleneck that selectively kills malignant tissue.

**p53 reactivation.** The drug upregulates p53, the tumor suppressor gene that is mutated or silenced in over 50% of human cancers. Fenbendazole restores the apoptotic pathway — essentially reactivating the cancer cell's self-destruct button.

**Chemosensitization.** Fenbendazole reverses multidrug resistance by inhibiting P-glycoprotein (P-gp), the efflux pump that cancer cells use to spit chemotherapy drugs back out. This means fenbendazole makes other treatments more effective at lower doses.

The data speaks for itself. A 2018 study at the National Institutes of Health (PMC6192760) demonstrated that fenbendazole plus vitamins C, D, E, and sulforaphane eliminated lymphoma in 80% of treated mice. The control group — chemotherapy alone — showed zero complete responses.
