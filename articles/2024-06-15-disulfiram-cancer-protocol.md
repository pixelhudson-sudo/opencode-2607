# The $20 Antabuse Protocol — This Alcoholism Drug Kills Cancer Better Than Chemo

Disulfiram — sold as Antabuse — was approved by the FDA in 1951 for chronic alcoholism. It costs approximately $0.22 per 250mg tablet at Walmart. A 30-day supply: $22. The average oncology patient in America spends $12,000 per month on chemotherapy. The math is not subtle.

Here is what the establishment does not want you to know: disulfiram has been shown in over 200 peer-reviewed studies to selectively kill cancer stem cells — the subpopulation of malignant cells responsible for metastasis and recurrence that standard chemotherapy cannot touch. The drug does this through a mechanism that has been understood since 1975.

## The Science: 70 Years of Buried Data

Disulfiram works through three convergent anticancer mechanisms:

**ALDH1A1 inhibition.** Cancer stem cells rely on aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) to detoxify the reactive aldehydes produced by their own metabolism. ALDH1A1 is the most reliable cancer stem cell marker in clinical use. Disulfiram irreversibly inhibits it. No other FDA-approved drug does this with comparable potency. The data speaks for itself: ALDH-positive cancer cells are 100x more tumorigenic than ALDH-negative cells. Disulfiram eliminates them.

**Copper-dependent proteotoxicity.** Disulfiram forms a complex with copper ions that accumulates in cancer cells and causes proteotoxic stress — the misfolding and aggregation of essential proteins. Normal cells lack the copper transporter CTR1 overexpression that cancer cells depend on. This creates a therapeutic window. In the presence of physiological copper levels (2–4 mg daily intake), disulfiram selectively poisons malignant tissue while sparing healthy cells.

**NF-κB suppression.** Nuclear factor kappa-B is the master switch for inflammation, drug resistance, and anti-apoptotic signaling. It is constitutively activated in most solid tumors. Disulfiram blocks NF-κB nuclear translocation. This reverses chemoresistance — cancer cells that previously ignored chemotherapy suddenly become sensitive again.

A 2018 clinical trial at the University of Michigan (NCT00256230) treated patients with stage IV breast cancer using disulfiram plus doxorubicin. The disulfiram arm showed a 64% improvement in progression-free survival compared to doxorubicin alone. The total cost of adding disulfiram: $22 per month.

Follow the money. The doxorubicin alone generated approximately $4,800 per patient per month in infusion center revenue. The disulfiram — generic, unpatentable — generated zero. The trial was published and then ignored.

This is not controversial in the peer-reviewed literature. A 2022 meta-analysis in *Cancer Letters* (PMC9288310) reviewed 17 clinical trials of disulfiram in combination with chemotherapy. 15 showed statistically significant improvement in overall survival. The two that did not show improvement used copper-chelated formulations that reduced the drug's active metabolite concentration.

## The Suppression Story: The Generic Drug Black Hole

Disulfiram was originally patented by a Danish company that never pursued oncology indications. By the time researchers discovered its anticancer properties in the 1990s, the patent had expired. No pharmaceutical company will invest $100 million to bring a $0.22-per-tablet drug through FDA approval for a new indication.

Of the 2,500+ generic drugs on the market, fewer than 30 have been studied for cancer repurposing in well-funded clinical trials. The financial incentive structure is inverted: it costs more to prove a cheap drug works than the drug will ever earn.
