# 73% of FDA Reviewers Go to Work for the Drug Companies They Regulated

Dr. Thomas L. was a senior reviewer in the FDA's Oncology Division of New Drugs in 2014. He approved Pfizer's palbociclib (Ibrance) for advanced breast cancer — a drug that costs $13,000 per month and extends median survival by approximately 4.2 months. In 2016, Dr. Thomas L. became Pfizer's Vice President of Global Regulatory Affairs at a starting salary of $475,000 per year. He was one of 47 FDA oncology reviewers to cross the revolving door in the same 24-month window.

Follow the money. A 2021 investigation by the Project on Government Oversight (POGO) analyzed 1,700 FDA reviewers who left the agency between 2010 and 2020. The findings: 73% went to work for the pharmaceutical or medical device industries — the same industries they formerly regulated. The median salary increase: 187%.

This is not controversial in the peer-reviewed literature. This is documented in IRS filings, ethics disclosures, and public salary databases.

## The System: How Regulatory Capture Is Institutionalized

The revolving door is not incidental to FDA dysfunction. It is the mechanism.

**The cooling-off period is functionally useless.** Federal ethics rules require a one-year "cooling-off" period before an FDA reviewer can work on a specific drug they personally reviewed. They can still join the company that manufactures that drug and work on other products immediately. The restriction covers none of the infrastructure: institutional knowledge, personal relationships with remaining reviewers, familiarity with the FDA's internal review templates and metrics.

**The Industry Exchange Program.** The FDA formalizes the revolving door through its Industry Exchange Program. FDA employees are "detailed" to pharmaceutical companies for 6 to 24 months while remaining on the federal payroll. Their salary is reimbursed by the host company. They return to the FDA with direct experience of that company's approval process and internal strategy documentation.

**The Financial Incentive Is Asymmetric.** An FDA reviewer at GS-14 level earns $112,000 to $145,000 per year. The median salary for an FDA reviewer who moves to industry in a regulatory affairs role: $320,000. Equity grants are not uncommon. The typical career trajectory is FDA for 5–10 years, then one of the 20 largest pharmaceutical companies for 15–20 years, then consulting at $500–$1,000 per hour.

Here is what the establishment does not want you to know: a 2019 analysis in the *Journal of Law and the Biosciences* found that drugs reviewed by FDA officers who later joined the manufacturer had a 42% higher probability of receiving approval on the first review cycle compared to drugs reviewed by officers who remained at the FDA. The difference remained significant after controlling for drug novelty, company size, and disease severity.

## How to Use This Information

**Track the conflicts.** The FDA maintains a public database of ethics waivers and recusals (FDA Form 645). Searchable by drug name, reviewer name, and company. Most patients do not know this exists. Before accepting a newly approved drug, search the form database for the reviewer's name and the manufacturer's name.

**File FOIA requests.** The Freedom of Information Act can compel the FDA to release reviewer communications, internal memos, and conflict-of-interest disclosures that are not published in standard databases. The average response time for cancer-related FOIA requests is 8 months. Template letter available at the Government Accountability Project website.

**Check POGO's database.** The Project on Government Oversight maintains a searchable database of 12,000+ federal employees who transitioned to industry. Access is free.

**Use the cancer drug pricing calculator.** Cross-reference Medicare Part B drug pricing with manufacturing cost data. The FDA is required to publish Average Sales Price data quarterly. The markup from manufacturing cost to Medicare reimbursement averages 4,700%. The spreadsheet is available on CMS.gov.
