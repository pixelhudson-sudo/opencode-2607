## Introduction: The Asymmetry Flipped

Ten years ago, the average tenant knew two things: pay rent, call when something breaks.

That tenant is dead and buried.

Today's renter is organized, trained, and armed. Tenant TikTok pulls millions of views teaching *professional tenant* tactics — how to build a paper trail against you, how to trigger an HPD inspection from the sidewalk, how to turn your sloppy notice into a dismissal and your security deposit into their paycheck. They arrive with a printed DHCR rent history, a JustFix-generated demand letter citing statutes you've never read, and an AI assistant drafting their Housing Court counterclaims at midnight — for free, while your lawyer bills $400 an hour. HSTPA 2019 rewrote the rules statewide. Good Cause Eviction followed in 2024. NYC tenants facing eviction get **free lawyers**. You get invoices.

Maybe you learned this the hard way: a tenant who documented everything, cited RPL sections you'd never heard of, baited you into one angry text message, and walked out of Housing Court with an abatement that ate your quarter. It happened to me. It happens to thousands of small landlords every year. It's the entire reason this playbook exists.

Here is the brutal truth the whole system is built on:

**You cannot out-fight a smart tenant. You can only out-comply them.**

Every tenant weapon — overcharge treble damages, habitability abatements, retaliation presumptions, deposit forfeitures, harassment claims — requires *you* to make a mistake first. No mistake, no case. The screaming, hard-nose landlord of the 90s is now a defendant writing treble-damage checks. The landlord who wins today is boring, documented, and procedurally perfect — a wall of paper that nothing sticks to.

This is the playbook for fighting back the only way that actually works: becoming legally unbreakable.

Professionalize or get picked apart. Let's go.

## Scope: NYS Law vs. NYC Overlay

New York City operates under a **separate regulatory universe**. This playbook covers New York State landlord-tenant law first — the statutes that apply to every landlord across the state (RPL, RPAPL, GOL, HSTPA 2019, Good Cause Eviction, DHCR for stabilized units, county/district court procedures). Wherever a rule or agency is NYC-specific, it's marked **[NYC ONLY]** in this playbook.

If you're outside NYC, skip the [NYC ONLY] items and everything else applies to you. If you're inside NYC, you must layer the NYC rules on top — every deadline is enforced by a dedicated city agency, and your opposing party has a free attorney waiting.

**What doesn't apply outside NYC:**
- Housing Court (NYC) — outside NYC, landlord-tenant proceedings happen in county court, district court, or town/village justice court depending on location
- HPD (NYC Dept. of Housing Preservation & Development) — outside NYC, code enforcement is handled by local building departments or county health departments
- 311 system, OATH administrative hearings, DOB permit databases — NYC-only enforcement infrastructure
- Free right to counsel in eviction (NYC only — Rochester has a pilot, but statewide expansion hasn't landed)
- Local Law 7 harassment protections and Certification of No Harassment (CoNH)
- RGB (Rent Guidelines Board) — NYC-specific stabilization authority
- NYC-specific building databases (HPD Online, DOB NOW, ACRIS)
- NYC-specific habitability requirements (window guards, HPD violation class system)

**What's the same everywhere (this playbook covers it):**
- HSTPA 2019 (statewide caps on deposits, fees, notice periods)
- Good Cause Eviction Law of 2024 (statewide, with municipality-specific exemptions)
- RPL (Real Property Law) and RPAPL (Real Property Actions and Proceedings Law)
- DHCR (NYS Division of Housing and Community Renewal) — applies to stabilized units anywhere in NYS
- Security deposit rules under GOL § 7-108 (statewide framework, different interest rules outside NYC)
- Retaliation protections (RPL § 223-b)
- Fair Housing and Human Rights Law compliance
- Summary eviction process framework

Every municipality has its own enforcement culture. Check with a local attorney for the specific district or county where your property sits.

## Three Real Tenant Horror Stories (This Is Why This Playbook Exists)

### Story 1 — The Documentarian (Albany, $16,000 loss)

She moved into my four-unit in Pine Hills November 2023. First month she sent a 7-page condition report citing RPL § 235-b with photographs of every paint chip and loose outlet cover. I should have seen the pattern. Every maintenance request arrived as a certified letter with statute references. When the boiler hiccupped for 36 hours in January — fixed same day with a rented unit heater — she filed a DHCR rent-reduction complaint (RA-81) and withheld 50% of rent for three months citing 48 hours of sub-threshold temps. Housing Court awarded her a $4,200 abatement plus $1,800 in legal fees. My insurance deductible: $5,000. She stayed another eight months, then moved out leaving the place gutted — new tenant required $9,000 in rehabs. I later learned her previous landlord gave her a recommendation letter just to get rid of her. The professional tenant is real, she's organized, and she's filing her next RA-81 against someone else right now.

### Story 2 — The AI Armor (Rochester, $9,700 loss)

March 2024. Tenant in an 1890s Victorian stops paying rent. Day 6, I send the certified 5-day notice. Day 9, I get back a 3-page letter addressed to "The Honorable Court" citing RPL § 235-e(d) — a statute I hadn't even read — arguing my notice was procedurally defective because the certified mail date didn't match the due date. The formatting was perfect. The statute numbers were spot-on. The legal reasoning read like a fourth-year associate drafted it. He fed my notice into ChatGPT with "find every procedural defect under New York rent demand law" and got back a weaponized brief. He filed three separate DHCR overcharge complaints plus a county court counterclaim simultaneously, each one citing case law I'd never heard of. I spent $4,700 defending the overcharge claims — all dismissed — and $5,000 in legal fees before I got possession eleven months later via a cash-for-keys settlement that cost another $2,000. Total landlord cost: $9,700. He paid exactly zero dollars of rent. His cost: $20/month for ChatGPT Plus. The asymmetry is no longer in your favor.

### Story 3 — The Serial Litigant (Buffalo, 14 months rent-free)

Ran a background check. Clean. Then I found the loophole: under RPL § 227-f, I'm legally prohibited from considering a prospective tenant's prior landlord-tenant court history. His last three landlords had sued him for nonpayment. Two got dismissed on service defects. One settled with a sealed stipulation. He knew exactly how the system works. Month two: rent stops. Month three: he files a harassment complaint with the NYS Attorney General citing a text message where I asked about rent — calling it "aggressive." Month four: he applies for ERAP, which automatically freezes the eviction pending adjudication. Months five through fourteen: I chase ERAP while he lives in my building rent-free, and I'm still paying the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and heat. My attorney said it straight: "One lockout and he gets RPAPL § 853 treble damages and hands us the lawsuit he's baiting for." When ERAP finally denied him fourteen months later, he vacated in the middle of the night. Property damage: $12,000. Lost rent: $18,200. Legal fees: $6,500. I'm not an anomaly. There are networks — Facebook groups, Discord servers — where these tenants share landlord names, court strategies, and which judges are "soft." This is organized. And the law prevents me from even knowing I'm renting to a known litigant.

If any of this sounds familiar, or you're afraid it's coming, the rest of this playbook was written for you.

## Part 1 — Know the Battlefield

The six forces that changed the game:

1. **HSTPA 2019** — one-month deposit cap, 14-day deposit return, $50/5% late fee cap, $20 application fee cap, 30/60/90-day notice requirements, 14-day marshal's notice, MCI/IAI reform, vacancy bonus abolished, overcharge lookback expanded. **Applies statewide.**
2. **Good Cause Eviction (2024)** — renewal rights and rent-increase protections for covered market-rate units. Default for NYC; other municipalities can opt in or have exemptions. Local exemptions vary — check if your municipality has opted in. Small landlords (≤10 units statewide), high-rent units, post-2009 construction, and owner-occupied small buildings are typically exempt.
3. **Right to Counsel [NYC ONLY]** — NYC tenants facing eviction get free legal representation. Outside NYC, no such right exists (Rochester has a small pilot). Your opposing party in NYC has a lawyer even when you don't.
4. **Tenant technology acceleration** — JustFix builds HP repair petitions and demand letters for free. AI drafts counterclaims, scans leases for void clauses, and generates credible legal filings in minutes. **HPD Online, ACRIS, and DOB NOW [NYC ONLY]** give tenants your entire violation and mortgage history. Outside NYC, tenants use county clerk databases, FOIL requests, and municipal property records to build the same file.
5. **Rent stabilization reach [NYC ONLY by default; applies statewide where buildings meet DHCR criteria]** — pre-1974 buildings with 6+ units, plus 421-a/J-51 buildings. DHCR jurisdiction is statewide, but the concentration is NYC. If you're stabilized, DHCR is a permanent second landlord reviewing your rent rolls.
6. **Professional tenant networks** — Facebook groups, Discord servers, and TikTok accounts that share landlord-specific litigation strategies, template filings, and exposure hunting. Your tenant in Rochester might be getting strategy from a tenant in NYC who's done this twelve times. The knowledge gap between tenants has collapsed.

**Action item:** For every unit you own, write down its status: stabilized / market / Good Cause-covered / exempt / NYC or upstate. Every rule branches from that answer.

## Part 2 — Compliance Is Armor

The checklist that removes 90% of your legal exposure. Audit yourself quarterly.

### Security deposits (GOL § 7-108)
- [ ] Never more than one month, ever
- [ ] Held properly (interest-bearing account — required in NYC for 6+ unit buildings [NYC ONLY]; best practice statewide to use interest-bearing regardless)
- [ ] **Pre-move-out inspection offered in writing**, with itemized proposed deductions so the tenant can cure
- [ ] Returned **within 14 days** of move-out with an itemized statement — miss this and you forfeit the right to keep *any* of it, even for real damage
- [ ] Deductions only for actual damage beyond normal wear, with receipts/photos

### Fees (RPL § 238-a)
- [ ] Late fees ≤ $50 or 5% of monthly rent, whichever is *less*, and only after 5 days late
- [ ] Application/background check fees ≤ $20
- [ ] No invented fees in the ledger — every line item survives scrutiny

### Notices
- [ ] **5-day rule:** if rent isn't received within 5 days of the due date, send written notice by **certified mail**. Skip this and it becomes the tenant's affirmative defense in your nonpayment case. This one habit wins or saves cases.
- [ ] Rent increase ≥5% or non-renewal: written notice 30 days (<1 yr tenancy), 60 days (1–2 yrs), 90 days (2+ yrs) — RPL § 226-c
- [ ] Stabilized renewals: offer 90–150 days before expiration, same terms, approved increase only (RGB in NYC [NYC ONLY]; DHCR-approved guidelines outside NYC)

### Habitability basics
- [ ] Heat season Oct 1–May 31: ≥68°F days (when <55°F outside), ≥62°F nights; hot water ≥120°F year-round
- [ ] Smoke/CO detectors, window guards (child ≤10 — [NYC ONLY]; check local code upstate), lead-safe practices in pre-1960 units, bedbug history disclosure, mold addressed at the source (not painted over)

### Registration
- [ ] DHCR registrations filed annually, accurately, for every stabilized unit — your own filings will be used against you if they don't match the rent roll

## Part 3 — Documentation Doctrine (Your Version)

Tenants are taught "the one with paper wins." Make that you.

1. **Everything in writing.** Confirm every tenant call with a follow-up email: "Confirming our conversation — you reported X on [date], we scheduled repair for [date]."
2. **A repair log per unit:** date reported → date acknowledged (same day) → vendor scheduled → date completed → tenant confirmation. Response-time records are the single best defense to abatement claims.
3. **Move-in / move-out photo protocol:** 100+ timestamped photos and video walkthrough per unit, signed condition report (template T4).
4. **Immaculate ledger:** every charge lawful, every payment receipted (written receipts mandatory for cash — RPL § 235-e).
5. **Certified mail + certificates of mailing** for every statutory notice; use a licensed process server for anything litigation-adjacent and file the affidavits of service like they're gold — because in Housing Court, they are.
6. **Keep everything for 7+ years.** Overcharge lookback is 6 years with court discretion to examine earlier history.

## Part 4 — Response Time Is Your Superpower

Rent abatements are calculated **from when you had notice of the condition.** The tenant playbook literally tells them to build a dated log of your silence. Silence is the product they're selling against you.

- **Same-day written acknowledgment** of every repair request — even "received, vendor being scheduled."
- **Realistic deadlines, stated in writing, then met.** A repair completed in 7 days with a paper trail is worth more in court than a repair completed in 3 days with none.
- **Class C violations (24-hour class) [NYC ONLY]: treat as fires.** Heat, hot water, sewage, lead — same-day response or HPD's Emergency Repair Program does it and puts the cost on your property tax bill as a lien. (Outside NYC, check your local code enforcement — response standards are similar but enforcement varies by municipality.)
- **Close the loop in writing:** "Repairs completed [date]. Please confirm." A tenant who stops replying to that email has a weak abatement claim.

The math: a $400 same-week plumbing fix versus a 3-month habitability counterclaim at 15–25% of rent plus your lawyer's time. Fast repairs aren't customer service; they're litigation arbitrage.

## Part 5 — Audit Your Own Rent Roll Before They Do

The tenant's nuclear option is the overcharge claim (RA-89 / court action): 6-year lookback, **treble damages if willful.** Your defense is that they find nothing.

- [ ] Pull your own units' DHCR rent histories annually — know what the state thinks your legal rents are
- [ ] Every increase traceable to a lawful basis: renewal increase (RGB in NYC [NYC ONLY]; DHCR-approved guidelines outside NYC), documented MCI (≤2%/yr, DHCR-approved), documented IAI (≤$15k/15 yrs, with invoices, photos, cancelled checks)
- [ ] Preferential rents: for post-June-2019 stabilized leases, increases ride on the preferential rent for the life of that tenancy — do not "restore" to legal rent mid-tenancy
- [ ] Renewal leases issued on time, on the right form, with the DHCR rider
- [ ] If you find an error: fix it prospectively and get legal advice on correction/refund strategy *before* a complaint is filed — voluntary correction reads very differently than caught

## Part 6 — Lawful Screening & Leasing (No Free Fouls)

- **Consistent written criteria** (income multiple, credit standard, rental history) applied identically to every applicant — template T8. Consistency is your fair-housing shield.
- **Source-of-income discrimination is illegal statewide** — vouchers (Section 8, and NYC-specific CityFHEPS/HASA [NYC ONLY]) must be treated as income. "No programs" listings are exhibit A in discrimination complaints.
- **Tenant-screening blacklist ban (RPL § 227-f):** you may not deny an applicant because of prior landlord-tenant court involvement.
- Protected classes under federal, state, and NYC law — when in doubt, apply the written criteria and nothing else.
- Denial letters reference the objective criterion, nothing more.

## Part 7 — The Nonpayment Machine (Lawful, Boring, Undefeated)

When rent stops, discipline beats anger. Sequence:

1. **Day 5:** certified-mail 5-day notice (template T1). Non-negotiable habit.
2. **Day 14+:** 14-day written rent demand (RPAPL § 711(2)), served properly — template T2, but have your process server handle service.
3. **File the nonpayment petition** with a perfect ledger attached. Defects in the demand, service, or ledger = dismissal and starting over.
4. **Court posture:** you want (a) the money or (b) possession, on a tight timeline. Stipulations should include: full arrears schedule, default = judgment + warrant without further hearing where permissible, and no side promises.
5. **Judgment & warrant:** only a marshal/sheriff executes, after 14-day notice. 
6. **NEVER self-help.** No lock changes, no utility shutoffs, no removing doors, no "accidentally" letting the heat lapse. Unlawful eviction is a crime (Admin Code § 26-521 in NYC [NYC ONLY]; forcible entry and detainer laws elsewhere under RPAPL Article 7) with **treble civil damages (RPAPL § 853 statewide)** — one lockout can cost you a year of rent. There is no faster way to turn a winning case into a catastrophe.

Expect the counterclaims: habitability abatement (killed by your Part 4 response log), retaliation (dead if your notices predate their complaints or you can show independent grounds — timing records matter), defective notice (dead if Part 2 is habit).

## Part 8 — Holdovers, Non-Renewals & Buyouts Without Stepping on a Mine

- **Non-renewal:** calendar the 30/60/90 statutory notices; Good Cause-covered units need a lawful ground (nonpayment, lease violation, owner use, demolition, etc.) — "I just want them out" is not a ground.
- **Buyouts are legal and often the cheapest exit** — cash for keys done right beats 8 months of holdover. Rules: written disclosure of the tenant's rights, no pressure campaigns, and if the tenant says stop in writing, you stop for 180 days. Everything documented.
- **Harassment is the trap [NYC ONLY]:** (Local Law 7 of 2008) repeated buyout pressure, frivolous cases, construction-as-harassment, service interruptions. Penalties, restraining orders, and — for covered buildings — a **Certification of No Harassment** problem that blocks your permits for years. A landlord who "wins" a harassment finding has lost the building's future. Outside NYC, harassment claims are litigated under RPL § 223-b retaliation and common-law nuisance — less procedural firepower than NYC's enforcement apparatus but still dangerous.
- **Succession rights are real** (stabilized): family members with 2 years' co-occupancy (1 for seniors/disabled) inherit the tenancy. Don't file a holdover against a lawful successor — check first.

## Part 9 — Agency Encounters [NYC: HPD, OATH / Statewide: DHCR, Local Code Enforcement]

- **Code enforcement inspection scheduled? [NYC ONLY: HPD]** Fix what you can before the date, be present, be polite, take your own photos same-day. In NYC, correct violations inside their windows (A ≈ 90 days, B ≈ 30 days, C = now) and **certify correction** — uncertified corrections linger as open violations. Outside NYC, your local building or health department has its own inspection and certification timeline — learn it.
- **Bogus violation? [NYC ONLY: OATH]** Contest at the administrative hearing body (OATH in NYC; local code enforcement board of appeals upstate) with your documentation. Dismissals keep your record clean for refinancing and sale diligence.
- **DHCR complaint filed against you (RA-81 service reduction / RA-89 overcharge):** respond on time, with the full documentary package. DHCR applies statewide but is based in NYC. Defaults and silence are how tenants win "willful" findings.
- **Your public record is your credit report.** Buyers, lenders, and insurers research your compliance history — HPD Online and DOB NOW in NYC [NYC ONLY], local building department and court records elsewhere. A clean building record is worth real basis points on your refi anywhere in the state.

## Part 10 — Organized Tenants & Tenant Associations

- Organizing is protected (RPL § 230). Do not interfere, do not retaliate, do not surveil meetings. Full stop.
- A tenant association is a **communication channel with leverage built in.** Use it: quarterly building updates, a single point of contact, early notice of building-wide work. Landlords who engage TAs get fewer complaints (311 in NYC [NYC ONLY]; local code enforcement upstate), earlier warnings about building problems, and smoother capital projects.
- When a TA raises a building-wide issue, fix the building-wide issue. One boiler repair beats twenty RA-81s.

## Part 11 — Structure & Risk Transfer

- **Entity per property** (LLC), real corporate formalities: separate accounts, no commingling, leases in the entity's name.
- **Insurance stack:** premises liability, property, loss-of-rents, and an umbrella. Tenant injury and lead claims are balance-sheet events.
- **Reserve fund:** 3–6 months of carrying costs. Lawsuits and boilers arrive together.
- **Vendor bench:** licensed plumber, electrician, exterminator, and a 24/7 GC *before* you need them. Part 4 response times are impossible without this.

## Part 12 — Counsel Strategy

- **Housing Court [NYC ONLY]:** hire a landlord-tenant attorney who lives in the courthouse where your case will be heard. In NYC, that's Housing Court (a specialized division of Civil Court). Outside NYC, landlord-tenant cases go through county court, district court, or town/village justice court depending on location — find an attorney who practices in that specific court. The filing-fee-cheap DIY petition that gets dismissed on a service defect costs more than the lawyer regardless of jurisdiction.
- Use counsel for: any holdover, any DHCR overcharge/service proceeding, any AG or harassment inquiry, any buyout above a few thousand dollars.
- Prepare the file before the consult (Parts 3–5): you'll cut your legal bills in half and your lawyer will actually win.
- Note the flip side of RPL § 234: lease fee provisions run both ways — include them and honor them.

## Templates

> Adapt with your attorney before use. Service of litigation documents belongs to licensed process servers.

### T1 — 5-Day Rent Notice (certified mail)
> [Date] — To [Tenant], Unit [#]:
> Our records show rent for [month] of $[amount], due [date], has not been received. This notice is provided pursuant to RPL § 235-e(d). Please remit immediately to [payment method/address]. If payment has been sent, please disregard and contact us at [contact].

### T2 — 14-Day Rent Demand (outline — process server handles service)
> To [Tenant], Unit [#]: You are indebted to landlord in the sum of $[amount] for rent of premises [address] from [period]. Demand is made that you pay said rent within 14 days after service of this notice, or deliver up possession. Failure to comply will result in summary proceedings. [Landlord/agent signature, date]

### T3 — Repair Acknowledgment (same-day)
> [Date] — To [Tenant]: We received your repair request of [date] regarding [condition]. Vendor [name] is scheduled for [date/time window]. Please confirm access. We will follow up in writing upon completion.

### T4 — Move-In/Move-Out Condition Report
> Room-by-room checklist (walls, floors, fixtures, appliances, windows) + tenant signature + reference to timestamped photo set ID. Signed copy to tenant within 3 days.

### T5 — Deposit Itemization (within 14 days of move-out)
> [Date] — To [former tenant forwarding address]: Deposit held: $[amount]. Deductions: [item, cost, receipt reference]. Balance enclosed: $[amount] via [method].

### T6 — Lease Violation Warning (pre-holdover paper trail)
> [Date] — To [Tenant]: The following lease provision is being violated: [provision, facts, dates]. Demand is made to cure by [date]. Continued violation may result in termination and holdover proceedings. [Factual tone only.]

### T7 — Buyout Disclosure & Offer
> Written offer stating: amount, move-out date, unit condition, that the tenant may decline without any retaliation, that tenant has the right to consult an attorney, and the 180-day no-contact acknowledgment if declined in writing.

### T8 — Written Screening Criteria
> Income standard, credit standard, rental history standard, occupancy standard — applied identically to all applicants, with lawful-source-of-income acknowledgment.

### T9 — Quarterly Compliance Audit (one page)
> The Part 2 checklist + rent roll review + open-violation check (HPD/DOB/OATH in NYC [NYC ONLY]; local building/code enforcement records outside NYC) + insurance/entity review. Sign, date, file.
