# The New York Tenant's Playbook
### Maximum Lawful Leverage Against Landlords — NYC-Focused, Statewide Where Noted

*General legal information, not legal advice for your specific case. For anything court-facing, use the free counsel resources in Part 9 — in NYC you often have a right to a free lawyer.*

The core doctrine: **your power comes from paper, process, and your landlord's legal exposure — never from confrontation.** Every tactic below is lawful. The "brutal" part is how much pain documented violations, fee-shifting statutes, and treble damages inflict on a landlord who misbehaves.

---

## Part 1 — Recon: Know Exactly What You Hold

Most tenants fight without knowing what unit they actually live in. Spend one hour on this first.

### 1.1 Determine your regulatory status
- **Request your apartment's rent history from DHCR** (NYS Homes & Community Renewal): call 718-739-6400 or visit a borough rent office with ID and proof of tenancy. Anyone can request their own unit's history — it shows every registered rent going back years and exposes overcharges instantly.
- **Rent-stabilized indicators:** NYC building built before 1974 with 6+ units, *or* any building that received 421-a/J-51 tax benefits. Check DHCR's registered building lists and the NYC Rent Guidelines Board site.
- **Market-rate?** You may still be covered by **Good Cause Eviction (2024)** if in NYC — renewal rights and protection against "unreasonable" increases (presumed unreasonable above the lower of 5% + CPI or 10%). Exemptions: small landlords (≤10 units statewide), high-rent units (245% of fair market rent), post-2009 construction, owner-occupied small buildings, and others.
- **Outside NYC:** HSTPA 2019 protections (deposits, fees, notices) apply statewide. Several upstate/local governments have opted into rent regulation (ETPA) or Good Cause — check your municipality.

### 1.2 Research your landlord like an adversary would
All free, all public record:
- **HPD Online** — every violation, complaint, and 311 history for your building
- **DOB NOW / BIS** — construction permits, stop-work orders, DOB violations
- **OATH/ECB** — environmental control board violations
- **ACRIS** — deeds, mortgages, who actually owns the building (useful for refinancing pressure points)
- **eCourts** — your landlord's litigation history across NY courts
- **DHCR building registration** — what the owner reported to the state

A building with open Class C violations, a pending refi, and a landlord with 40 Housing Court appearances is a landlord with no appetite for a documented fight. That's your opening position.

### 1.3 Assemble your file
- Lease + all renewals + riders (stabilized leases *must* include the DHCR rider)
- Every rent receipt, bank record of payment, ledger
- Every communication with the landlord/management, oldest to newest
- Photos/video of every condition issue, dated
- The rent history from 1.1

## Part 2 — Documentation Doctrine (This Is the Whole Game)

Judges and agencies believe paper, not stories. Rules:

1. **Everything in writing.** Follow every phone call with an email: "Confirming our call today in which you said ___." Keep tone flat and factual — your letters will be read by a judge someday. Write every one as Exhibit A.
2. **Certified mail, return receipt** (or email + letter) for anything important. Keep receipts.
3. **Photo/video everything**, timestamped, wide shot + close-up, repeated over time to show duration.
4. **Keep a log** (template in Part 8): date, issue, who you contacted, response, deadline given, outcome.
5. **Pay rent on time, always, via traceable method.** If you pay cash, you are legally entitled to a written receipt — demand it every time (RPL § 235-e).
6. **Know the 5-day rule:** if rent isn't received within 5 days of due date, the landlord must send you written notice by certified mail. Their failure is an affirmative defense in any nonpayment case (RPL § 235-e(d)).

## Part 3 — The Escalation Ladder (Repairs & Habitability)

Every NY residential lease contains an **implied warranty of habitability (RPL § 235-b)** — heat, hot water, working plumbing/electric, no leaks/mold/infestation, safe common areas. It's non-waivable. Escalate in order; each step multiplies the next step's force.

### Step 1 — Written repair request
Short, factual, dated, with a reasonable deadline (7–14 days; 24 hours for emergencies). Template in Part 8.

### Step 2 — Call 311 / HPD (NYC)
Free city inspection. Violations get classified:
- **Class A** (non-hazardous) — ~90 days to correct
- **Class B** (hazardous) — ~30 days
- **Class C** (immediately hazardous — no heat/hot water, lead hazards, severe leaks) — 24 hours/immediately

Violations become **public record**, which poisons the landlord's refinancing, sale, and insurance diligence. If the owner ignores Class C violations, **HPD's Emergency Repair Program does the work and bills the owner as a property tax lien.** You just made the city your contractor.

### Step 3 — Formal demand letter citing law
Reference RPL § 235-b, the HPD violation numbers, and state you'll pursue all remedies: HP action, rent abatement, DHCR complaint. Template in Part 8.

### Step 4a — HP Action (Housing Court)
Tenant-initiated case to compel repairs. Free to file, fast, and HPD inspects for the court. Judges order repairs with deadlines and civil penalties. **JustFix.nyc has a free tool that builds the HP filing for you.**

### Step 4b — Rent abatement (the money remedy)
Breach of the warranty of habitability entitles you to a **rent reduction retroactive to when the landlord knew of the condition.** Asserted as a counterclaim in Housing Court or demanded in negotiation. Documented months of no heat = real money.

### Step 4c — DHCR service-reduction complaint (stabilized units — the silent killer)
File **RA-81 (decreased services)**. If DHCR finds reduced services, it **freezes/reduces your rent at the prior level until services are restored** — the landlord cannot collect any increase. This is one of the most feared outcomes for stabilized landlords.

### Extreme option — 7A proceeding
For buildings with conditions dangerous to life/health/safety, 1/3+ of tenants can petition Housing Court to appoint a **7A administrator** who takes the building away from the landlord entirely (RPAPL § 770). Rare, devastating, real.

### Use with caution (get advice first)
- **Rent withholding:** NY has no escrow statute. Withholding without a plan risks a nonpayment case. If you do it, put 100% of the rent into a separate untouched account and talk to a lawyer/tenant org first. Done right (with habitability defense), it's powerful; done sloppy, it hands the landlord a case.
- **Repair and deduct:** exists via case law (breach of warranty), not a clean statute. Keep receipts, use licensed contractors, expect a fight. Lawyer first.

### Fast track — heat & hot water
NYC heat season is **Oct 1 – May 31**: 6am–10pm must be ≥68°F indoors when it's <55°F outside; 10pm–6am must be ≥62°F. Hot water ≥120°F year-round. No heat = immediate 311, immediate Class C, immediate Emergency Repair eligibility. Log indoor temps twice daily with a cheap thermometer photo next to a dated phone.

## Part 4 — Money Leverage

### Security deposit (GOL § 7-108)
- Capped at **one month**, statewide.
- Must be returned **within 14 days** of move-out with an itemized statement of any deductions — **miss the deadline and the landlord forfeits the right to keep any of it.**
- You have the right to a **pre-move-out inspection** with an itemized list of proposed deductions and a chance to cure. Request it in writing.
- Buildings with 6+ units: deposit must sit in an interest-bearing account; you're owed the interest (minus 1% admin).
- Shortchanged? **Small Claims Court — up to $10,000 in NYC.** Bring the file from Part 1.3. These cases are nearly unwinnable for landlords who blew the 14-day rule.

### Junk fees (RPL § 238-a)
- Late fees capped at **$50 or 5% of monthly rent, whichever is less** — and only after 5 days' lateness.
- Application/background check fees capped at **$20** (HSTPA).
- Illegal fees in your ledger = deductions from any claim they make against you, and evidence of bad faith.

### Rent overcharge (stabilized units — the nuclear option)
- Compare your rent history (Part 1.1) against legal increases. Any unexplained jump, missing registration, or "preferential rent" games = potential overcharge.
- File **RA-89 with DHCR** or sue in court: **6-year lookback** for refunds, and **treble damages for willful overcharge.** Courts may examine history beyond 6 years to determine the legal rent. Landlords settle hard and fast when faced with treble-damage math.
- **MCI increases** (building-wide improvements) are capped at 2%/year and challengeable at DHCR — demand the filing and receipts. **IAI increases** (in-unit) capped at $15,000 per 15 years.
- **Preferential rent:** for post-HSTPA stabilized leases, the preferential (discounted) rent is the base for all future increases **for the duration of your tenancy.** If they raised you off the old legal rent, that's actionable.
- Stabilized tenants are entitled to a **renewal lease 90–150 days before expiration**, on the same terms, at RGB-set increases. No renewal offered = DHCR complaint.

### Notice rights (market-rate units, RPL § 226-c)
Rent increase of 5%+ *or* non-renewal requires written notice: **30 days** (<1 yr tenancy), **60 days** (1–2 yrs), **90 days** (2+ yrs). Defective notice = the increase/non-renewal fails.

## Part 5 — Eviction Defense

Only a judge can evict you. Only a marshal/sheriff can execute a warrant. Anything else is a crime.

1. **Never ignore papers.** In NYC nonpayment cases you typically have ~10 days to answer after service. Missing deadlines converts winnable cases into defaults.
2. **Right to counsel:** NYC tenants facing eviction get **free legal representation** (income-based full rep; brief advice for everyone) — call 311 and ask for the Office of Civil Justice / Right to Counsel, or contact Legal Services NYC, Legal Aid, or NYLAG. Use it. Representation rates flip outcomes.
3. **Your defense arsenal:** warranty of habitability counterclaims (abatement!), retaliation defense (RPL § 223-b — presumption of retaliation within 1 year of protected activity like complaints/organizing), defective notices (Parts 4), the 5-day certified-mail rule, rent-demand defects (14-day written demand required), acceptance-of-rent waivers, laches.
4. **Stipulations:** most cases settle via stip in Housing Court. A good stip is a weapon: repairs with hard deadlines, abatement credits, payment plans, penalties for default. **Never sign a stip you haven't fully read — it ends your case on whatever terms are in it.** Get the free lawyer to read it.
5. **Marshal's notice:** after a warrant, you get **14 days' notice** before eviction. Even then, an Order to Show Cause can pull you back into court.
6. **Illegal lockout?** Call 911 immediately — unlawful eviction is a crime (NYC Admin Code § 26-521). Then file an illegal-lockout case in Housing Court to be restored to possession, and sue for **treble damages under RPAPL § 853.** Also photograph everything, keep proof of occupancy handy (ID, mail, lease).

## Part 6 — Collective Power (Multiply Everything)

- **Right to organize (RPL § 230):** tenants may form/join tenant associations, hold meetings in common areas, distribute literature. Protected activity — retaliation is illegal.
- **Tenant associations** file coordinated HP actions, DHCR service-reduction complaints, and overcharge cases. Twenty simultaneous RA-81s is a landlord's worst quarter.
- **Buyouts:** legal to accept, but the power is knowing you don't have to. Repeated buyout pressure after you say no = **harassment.** Put "do not contact me about a buyout" in writing — they must stop for 180 days.
- **Succession rights (stabilized):** family members (defined broadly, including non-traditional family) who lived with the tenant 2 years (1 year if senior/disabled) before vacancy/death inherit the tenancy. Landlords "forget" this constantly.
- **Harassment claims:** NYC tenants can sue landlords in Housing Court for harassment (Local Law 7 of 2008) — restraining orders plus civil penalties, and findings can block a landlord's future permits via Certification of No Harassment requirements.
- **Roommates (RPL § 235-f):** your right to share the apartment can't be lease-waived. **Subletting (RPL § 226-b):** consent can't be unreasonably withheld in 4+ unit buildings. **Pets:** NYC pet law waives no-pet clauses if the landlord knew of an openly kept pet for 3+ months and didn't act.
- **Screening blacklist banned (RPL § 227-f):** a landlord can't deny you housing for having been in landlord-tenant proceedings.

## Part 7 — Negotiation: Turning Leverage into Outcomes

The leverage map, ranked by pain to landlord:

1. **Treble damages** — willful overcharge, illegal eviction. Six-figure exposure on paper.
2. **Fee-shifting (RPL § 234)** — if the lease gives the landlord attorney's fees, you get the same right. Their lease, your free lawyer bills.
3. **Public violations** — HPD/DOB records that kill refinancings, sales, insurance.
4. **DHCR orders** — service-reduction rent freezes that last until they comply.
5. **HPD Emergency Repair liens** — the city fixes it and taxes them.
6. **Organized filings** — coordinated complaints across a building.
7. **AG attention** — the NY Attorney General pursues portfolio landlords for harassment/overcharge patterns; your documented file is a referral.

Rules of engagement:
- **Sequence, don't shotgun.** Request → agency → demand → filing. Each skipped step weakens you; each completed step is a ratchet.
- **Ask for everything documented:** full repairs + abatement for affected months + fees waived + written confirmation. Anchor high on the abatement percentage.
- **Get it in writing.** Settlements, repair schedules, credits — written, signed, dated. Verbal promises are air.
- **Deadlines with consequences:** "If not completed by [date], I will file [specific action] on [date+1]." Then do it. Credibility compounds.
- **Stay ice-cold polite.** The file you're building is either your shield or their rope, depending on your tone.

## Part 8 — Hard Rules (Stay Responsible)

- Pay rent on time via traceable methods; never hand the landlord a clean nonpayment case.
- Never fabricate or exaggerate conditions — one lie poisons a hundred true pages.
- No self-help: don't change locks on them, don't block their access for lawful repairs, don't touch their property.
- Don't withhold rent or repair-and-deduct without advice and a funded escrow.
- Never miss a court deadline; never sign a stip or buyout unread.
- Keep every interaction lawful and boring. The boring party wins in Housing Court.

## Templates

### T1 — Repair Request (Step 1)
> **[Your name / unit / date]**
> To [landlord/management]:
> This letter requests repair of the following conditions in my apartment: [list, with dates first observed]. These conditions affect habitability. Please complete repairs by [date — 7–14 days]. I am available for access [windows]. Please confirm receipt and your repair schedule in writing.

### T2 — Formal Demand (Step 3)
> **[Name / unit / date — sent certified mail]**
> To [landlord]:
> On [dates] I requested repair of [conditions]; HPD inspection on [date] resulted in violations [numbers/classes]. The conditions remain uncorrected. They constitute a breach of the warranty of habitability under RPL § 235-b and applicable code provisions. Demand is made for completion of all repairs by [date]. If not completed, I will pursue all remedies without further notice, including an HP action, a rent abatement claim retroactive to [date of notice], a DHCR complaint for reduction in services [if stabilized], and all available penalties and fees under RPL § 234. Govern yourself accordingly.

### T3 — Security Deposit Demand
> **[Name / former unit / date]**
> I vacated [unit] on [date]. Under GOL § 7-108 my deposit of $[amount] was due within 14 days with an itemized statement of any deductions. [No return/statement has been received / The deductions of $X are disputed as pre-existing/normal wear — see attached move-in and move-out photos.] Your failure to comply forfeits any right to retain the deposit. Return $[amount] by [date — 7 days] or I will file in Small Claims Court seeking the deposit plus costs.
